Why "Solar System"?

Countless planetary systems, with planets orbiting a central star, populate the universe like ours. We call our system 'the solar system', using 'solar' from 'solis', Latin for Sun, to describe Sun-related matters.

Universal Law of Gravitation

Various planetary systems, each with planets revolving around a main star, exist in the cosmos. Despite their differences, they all follow the universal law of gravitation with the central star as the gravitational fulcrum.

Decoding 'Solar' in Solar System

"Our 'solar' system, a specific grouping of celestial bodies, underscores the Sun's importance in our existence. The term 'solar', from the Latin 'solis' meaning Sun, encapsulates all Sun-related phenomena, from its nuclear-powered glow to its gravitational impact.

The Unique Perspective of Our Solar System

Though our solar system is one among countless in the universe, its distinctive composition, Earth's presence, and life-supporting capacity under the Sun's ('solis') influence provide a unique framework for studying other systems and understanding the wider universe.

Cosmic Dimensions

Beyond the eight planets that encircle the Sun, our solar system extends much further. One significant region is the Kuiper Belt, positioned beyond Neptune's orbit. The Kuiper Belt comprises a vast expanse of icy bodies, with most being smaller than the renowned Kuiper Belt Object, the dwarf planet Pluto.

NASA's New Horizons spacecraft captured this high-resolution enhanced color view of Pluto on July 14, 2015.

The Oort Cloud is a vast spherical shell surrounding our solar system, extending beyond the Kuiper Belt. While never directly observed, its existence is supported by mathematical models and observations of comets originating from this distant region.

Comprised of icy debris, some as large as mountains, the Oort Cloud orbits the Sun at an astonishing distance of up to 1.6 light-years away. It stretches from 5,000 to 100,000 astronomical units (AU), with one AU being the distance from the Sun to Earth (about 93 million miles or 150 million kilometers). This vast shell marks the boundary of the Sun's gravitational influence, allowing orbiting objects to reverse course and return closer to our Sun.
