Pluto Chronicles

Pluto, a dwarf planet, resides in the Kuiper Belt - a region filled with icy bodies beyond Neptune's orbit. This distant realm of our solar system may contain millions of these objects, often referred to as Kuiper Belt objects (KBOs) or trans-Neptunian objects (TNOs).

Despite being smaller than Earth's Moon, Pluto hosts fascinating features, including a Texas-sized, heart-shaped glacier. It has blue skies, towering mountains, spinning moons, and even experiences snowfall - with the snow being red.

On July 14, 2015, NASA’s New Horizons spacecraft undertook a historic journey through the Pluto system. It provided the first close-up images of Pluto and its moons, offering insights that have significantly enhanced our understanding of these enigmatic worlds on the solar system's frontier.

Fact's about Pluto

DWARF DOMINION

Pluto, once considered the ninth planet, is now classified as a dwarf planet.

KUIPER KING

It's the largest object in the Kuiper Belt, a region of the solar system beyond Neptune filled with small bodies and dwarf planets.

YEARLY JOURNEY

Pluto's orbit around the Sun takes approximately 248 Earth years.

TILTED TERRAIN

It has a highly inclined and eccentric orbit that takes it from 30 to 49 Astronomical Units (AU) from the Sun.

ICY HEART

Pluto's most famous feature is a heart-shaped region named Tombaugh Regio, home to nitrogen and methane ices.

MOON MYSTERY

Its largest moon, Charon, is so big that Pluto and Charon are sometimes considered a binary system.

SURFACE SECRETS

Pluto's surface is composed of a mixture of rock and ice, with a variety of terrains ranging from plains to mountain ranges.

FRIGID FRONTIER

It has an extremely cold climate, with surface temperatures averaging around -375 to -400 degrees Fahrenheit (-225 to -240 degrees Celsius).
